After my most recent 2 day trip to Traverse City, touring wine country, driving down M-22 and again seeing the breathtaking vistas of Western Michigan, I'm already planning my next trip there.

---

# Michigan Summers

In the middle of August, I'll be moving to New York for a month.

It's something I've wanted to do for a while. Bigger city, better public transit, more people, more opportunities, and a chance to find out what living there actually feels like instead of wondering from afar.

I'm excited for it.

What I didn't expect was that planning the trip would make me think more about Michigan than New York.

Most people think of Michigan as the state with long winters, college football, and a lot of snow. They're not wrong, but that's only half the story. If you've only experienced Michigan between November and March, it's hard to understand why people stay.

Then summer arrives.

The trees fill back in, lakes are packed with boats, restaurant patios come alive, and weekends suddenly feel like they belong outside. After months of gray skies, summer almost feels earned.

That's what was on my mind when we drove up to Traverse City over the Fourth of July weekend.

The drive along M-22 through the Leelanau Peninsula ended up being my favorite part of the trip. Every few miles there was another overlook, another beach, another vineyard, or another excuse to pull over "for just a minute."

It's one of those roads where getting somewhere almost feels secondary to everything you pass along the way.

Lake Michigan has a strange effect on me.

People call it a lake, but standing on the shoreline doesn't really feel like standing next to one. You can't see the other side. The water stretches out until it blends into the sky, and for a moment it's easy to forget you're nowhere near an ocean.

---

<Figure src='/assets/images/traverse-city-summer/lake.jpeg' alt="Lake Michigan"/>

Somewhere along that shoreline I kept thinking about the saying, *the grass is always greener on the other side.*

Standing there, there isn't really another side to imagine. Just water stretching until it disappears into the horizon.

I've spent a lot of time this year thinking about what life would be like somewhere else. Whether that's New York or another city down the road, it's easy to imagine that everything gets a little more exciting once you leave home.

Maybe it does.

At the same time, weekends like this remind me how easy it is to overlook the places you've always had nearby.

Growing up in Michigan, Traverse City, Sleeping Bear Dunes, and the Leelanau Peninsula never felt urgent. They were always only a few hours away. We'd get around to them eventually.

The funny thing is, we rarely did.

Somehow the places furthest from home always seemed worth planning for, while the ones in our own backyard quietly stayed on the "someday" list.

---

<Figure src='/assets/images/traverse-city-summer/vineyard.jpeg' alt="Mari Vineyards"/>

---

This year has been different.

Instead of treating weekends as time to recover before Monday, I've tried to spend more of them exploring. Some trips have taken me across the country. Others have been just a few hours from home. Looking back, I don't think Michigan changed. I just stopped assuming there would always be another summer to see it.

Traverse City ended up being a good reminder of that.

The wineries were great, downtown was full of energy because of the Cherry Festival, and watching the fireworks over Grand Traverse Bay was a perfect way to end the evening.

Those are the moments that show up in photos.

The ones I'll probably remember most are the quieter parts of the weekend. Driving with the windows down. Pulling over because a view looked interesting. Walking through small towns without checking the time or thinking about the next stop.

Those moments don't make it onto an itinerary, but they're usually the reason a trip sticks with me.

I'll still be in New York in a few weeks, and I'm looking forward to it.

At the same time, I know I'll be trading away a few of the best weeks of a Michigan summer. Before long the evenings will get a little cooler, the leaves will start hinting at changing color, and then, before we know it, another winter will be here.

Maybe that's why this trip stayed with me.

Not because it changed my mind about leaving, but because it reminded me why leaving isn't an easy decision in the first place.

I still don't know where I'll end up living long-term. Maybe it'll be Michigan. Maybe it'll be somewhere completely different.

What I do know is that I don't want to make the mistake of always looking toward what's next without appreciating what's already here.

This summer has been the first where I feel like I've genuinely gotten to know my home state instead of just living in it. I only wish I'd started doing that sooner.
