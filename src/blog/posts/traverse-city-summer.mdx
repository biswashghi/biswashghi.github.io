After a recent two-day trip to Traverse City, touring wine country, driving down M-22, and again taking in the sweeping vistas of Western Michigan, I've found a change in the life I want to live.

---

In the next couple months, I want to move to NYC. Not due to some long held dream of living it up in the Big Apple, but more as a way of pushing myself to do more.
I've enjoyed a stable post-college life here in Michigan with family for the past 6 years. I've made some great friends, traveled a lot with family, and have had a very enjoyable time.

However, in this time here in Michigan, I've gotten comfortable. That comfort has started to show up as a lack of energy to try for something harder and push myself to do better.

I never intended this trip to Traverse City to be anything special or to have anything to do with this goal, but after this 3 day trip, I think I've found some threads that connect these two in ways I never thought before.

---

Western Michigan is a magical place... well, at least between June and September. The vista of the lake and the beaches is always stunning no matter how many times you experience it, which makes it even more sad that I've not experienced it as much as I should have.

Not having been there in a while, the most surprising thing I found was the distinct parts of it that I had never really paid attention to before. The wineries and vines across the peninsula north of Traverse City were stunning in person. Taking a seat overlooking vineyards and watching the bay was a memory I don't think I'll ever forget. Driving through M-22 and passing the rows of cherry trees, and finally understanding where the cherries we were about to see in the festival were coming from, was another highlight.

Outside of the vineyards and the cherry trees, though, the drive itself was just as memorable. The rolling low hills, the quintessential Midwest red barns, old houses, and fields of empty space gave off a sense of calm that feels hard to find elsewhere.

Interspersed between the calm drive and the cultivated fields, the towns themselves were a highlight. Leland, Northport, and Suttons Bay were the kind of places where I can imagine myself spending future summers just relaxing.

This was also the weekend of July 4th. The 100th Cherry Festival was spinning up, and there were already large plans for the 250th fireworks. Both gave the trip an excitement I don't think I've felt in a while. The cherry festival itself wasn't much to write home about, but the 250th fireworks, drone show, and pyrotechnics were an exciting end to a memorable weekend trip. As someone pointed out to me, "[250 years], that's a special number".

250 years is special, it showcases the strength of the institution built to support a country like this, and throughout this time I think the country has seen the founding principles of why allowing this to be a place for others to come and grow has made it all the more successful. I myself have gained much from being here and can't ever take it for granted, and I hope this institution can survive these testing times to come out even stronger and thrive for another 250 years.

And somewhere between the fireworks, the lake, and the long drives back, the whole weekend began to feel like more than just a summer trip.

After this weekend, I felt an even stronger conviction to move somewhere. The quietness of the drive, the lakes, and the dunes made me reflect on the past several years and the choices I've made. What Michigan provides in the summer is unparalleled, but in those moments I could already imagine a future version of me looking back and wondering why I took this part of my life for granted. I've had the chance to get to this point, but I still have room to aim even higher.

Life has become too easy for me, and I think that gives me a chance to try something harder. If I do end up in NYC or somewhere else in the next few months, this will be one of the moments that helped me make that decision. Hopefully I succeed even further by moving somewhere that will test me, but if I don't, it still brings some peace knowing that Michigan, my home, is always there to welcome me back.
