Before 2020, my family didn't really take vacations.

We traveled, but almost all of those trips revolved around visiting relatives. Vacations in the traditional sense were rare. Between work, school, finances, and everyone's schedules, there just wasn't much opportunity for them.

That slowly changed after I graduated.

My sister and I started working full-time, my parents had a little more freedom, and for the first time it felt like we had both the time and the means to travel simply because we wanted to. Over the past few years we've taken a handful of trips together, each one a little different from the last. I'll probably write about some of those later, but our cruise through the Bahamas stands out for a different reason.

It made me realize that family vacations feel completely different once everyone has grown up.

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/family-selfie.jpeg' alt="Family on the cruise"/>

Cruises are almost perfectly designed for that stage of life.

Nobody has to drive.

Nobody has to plan tomorrow's route.

Nobody has to decide which restaurant we're eating at or worry about checking into another hotel.

Once you're on board, everyone gets to enjoy the trip however they want.

Most mornings we'd eat breakfast together before naturally splitting up for a few hours. Sometimes I'd head to the upper deck with a book or just watch the ocean. My parents might wander around the ship or find a show they wanted to watch. By dinner we'd almost always end up back together, comparing what each of us had done that day.

It never felt like we had to spend every minute together.

Somehow that made the time we did spend together feel even better.

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/drink.jpeg' alt="Relaxing on the cruise deck"/>

One afternoon I decided to try the rock climbing wall, mostly because I'd never climbed one on a moving ship before.

The wind made it harder than I expected, but the view from the top made the climb worthwhile.

The rest of the week was much less adventurous.

I spent far more time than I expected simply sitting on deck watching the ocean.

Normally I'd get restless after ten or fifteen minutes. Here, I could easily lose track of an hour. There wasn't really anything I needed to be doing, and after a couple of days I stopped feeling like I should be doing something else instead.

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/selfie-solo.jpeg' alt="Watching the ocean"/>

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/rock-wall.jpeg' alt="Rock climbing on the cruise"/>

The ship itself also surprised me.

The theater productions were genuinely impressive, and we ended up seeing more shows than I'd expected. In the evenings we'd usually find ourselves listening to live music in one of the lounges while looking out through giant windows at the ship's wake disappearing into the darkness.

Those evenings ended up becoming part of the routine.

Dinner.

A show.

Live music.

A walk around the deck.

Then doing it all again the next day.

It sounds repetitive, but I think that's exactly why it worked.

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/theater.jpeg' alt="Theater show"/>

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/cruise-night.jpeg' alt="Night view from the lounge"/>

When we reached Nassau, we explored the city the same way we'd approached the rest of the trip.

Without much of a plan.

Umbrella Lane was worth seeing, if only because it's exactly as colorful as it looks in every photo. We wandered through the Queen's Staircase, spent some time near the beach, and walked wherever looked interesting.

I found myself enjoying the quieter streets more than the busy tourist areas.

I've noticed that's becoming a pattern whenever I travel. Give me somewhere walkable, somewhere scenic, and enough time to wander without watching the clock, and I'm usually happy.

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/umbrella.jpeg' alt="Umbrella Lane"/>

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/queen-steps.jpeg' alt="Queen's Staircase"/>

<Figure src='/assets/images/cruise-bahamas/beach.jpeg' alt="Beach in Nassau"/>

Looking back, what stays with me isn't really the Bahamas.

It's watching how naturally our family fits together on trips now.

I still want to come back and do another cruise with friends someday. I'm sure it'd be a completely different experience.

But I'm glad my first cruise was with my family.

Somewhere between sitting on the upper deck doing absolutely nothing, wandering through Nassau, and ending every evening around the dinner table, I realized these trips have quietly become less about where we're going and more about the chance to spend time together while everyone still can.
